From the Editor

The Bubble We Built Cannot Be Passed Down

Our parents came to America, worked harder than anyone around them, and constructed something extraordinary — a bubble of professional achievement and community comfort. Engineers, physicians, business owners. Quiet, productive, and almost entirely absent from the room where American policy is decided.

That bubble worked for one generation. It will not protect the next.

"In 2023–24, Hindu Americans collectively invested less than two cents per person in political infrastructure. That is not modesty. That is absence."

Policy is already being written about us. Legislation has been introduced — in California, New York, and at the federal level — that frames Hindu identity as a liability in workplaces and on campuses. Caste discrimination frameworks built on activist-funded surveys are already embedded in HR policy at major American corporations. Temple attacks trend on WhatsApp for 48 hours and disappear with zero political consequence.

This is not alarmism. It is documented. The pages in this edition lay it out institution by institution, bill by bill, number by number.

A4H — Americans for Hindus — is the organization working to close this gap. It is the only Hindu Super PAC in America. It operates on a volunteer basis. Every dollar goes toward funding candidates who understand what is at stake for our community.

What follows is the case for why it matters, and what you can do about it. I wrote this because I believe the data is more persuasive than any appeal to identity — and because the people in my community who need to act are the ones who respond to evidence, not emotion.

The Issues

What Is Already Happening to Our Community

These are not distant threats. They are policies already on the books, bills that nearly became law, and practices embedded in workplaces right now.

This is not like India's reservation system. In India, caste classification comes with government benefits — reserved seats in jobs and education. In America, there are no such benefits. None. Caste is being added purely as a discrimination category — meaning any Hindu or Indian employee can be accused of caste bias by a colleague, triggering an HR investigation, with no offsetting advantage whatsoever. Vague definitions make it easier to file complaints, not harder. You do not need to know someone's actual caste to accuse them.

California's SB403 was authored by Aisha Wahab — an Afghan-American Muslim state senator — and co-authored by Ash Kalra, an Indian-American Hindu assemblyman. The organization behind it, Equality Labs, ran over 700 lobbying meetings in a single legislative cycle. Governor Newsom vetoed it in 2023. They have publicly stated they will bring it back.

Meanwhile, Cisco, Brandeis, Brown, and multiple UC campuses have already embedded caste policies into HR and student conduct frameworks — no legislation needed.

At Michigan, Harvard, and Columbia, mandatory diversity training frames Hindu practice as a social hierarchy to be dismantled. Your child is navigating a campus where their identity is a liability. State legislatures control what is taught in K–12 classrooms. PAC funding of the right candidates is how that changes.

Our sacred swastika — used in rangoli, temples, and weddings for 5,000 years — is being equated to the Nazi Hakenkreuz in state legislation. These are two different symbols with different names, origins, and meanings. Without our voice in the drafting room, these bills will never make that distinction.

Virginia HB-2783 (2025) is the counter-example: it explicitly protected the Hindu swastika while classifying the Nazi hakenkreuz as a Class 6 felony. That passed because Hindu Americans organized and showed up. It is proof the model works.

Political attention follows political organization. When other communities face attacks on their places of worship, organized lobbying infrastructure ensures swift legislative response. We have no equivalent. Every attack trends on WhatsApp for 48 hours and then disappears with zero political consequence for anyone.

In California, anti-Hindu hate ranked second only to antisemitism for four consecutive years — with four temples vandalized between December 2023 and March 2025, and no arrests.

The next crisis will not announce itself. Without pre-built legislative relationships and rapid-response capacity, we will react the same way we always have — too late and too scattered to make any politician pay a price. A4H is building that infrastructure now, before the next crisis hits.

The Evidence

Named Institutions. Documented Actions.

If you think these are distant or theoretical — they are not. The following institutions have already acted. These are documented policies and legislative records, not projections.

Already Law or Policy

A federal lawsuit revealed Cisco discriminated against a Dalit engineer — and internal HR policy treated caste as a protected category, effectively placing Hindu and Indian employees under a presumption of guilt. This policy is already in place and has set a national template now replicated across Silicon Valley.

Seattle became the first U.S. city to add caste as a protected category in anti-discrimination law in 2023. The ordinance passed with no meaningful Hindu voice in the room — because we had no political relationships with the council. More cities are watching.

Brandeis has added caste as a protected category in its official non-discrimination and harassment policies. The standard used is based on activist frameworks that treat Hindu identity as inherently connected to caste oppression.

Brown has implemented caste protections. The standard used is based on activist frameworks that treat Hindu identity as inherently connected to caste oppression. Hindu students had no representation when these policies were adopted.

Legislative Success — Proof of Concept

Virginia HB-2783 passed in 2025, explicitly distinguishing the Hindu swastika from the Nazi hakenkreuz in state law. This bill passed because Hindu Americans organized, showed up, and had relationships with legislators in advance. It is direct proof that the model works — and that the same model is needed everywhere else.

Vetoed — Campaign Continues

SB403 would have added caste discrimination to California state law. Vetoed by Governor Newsom in 2023 after sustained advocacy — and its architects have stated publicly they will reintroduce it.

The organization behind SB403 is Equality Labs — funded through the Ford Foundation, the Omidyar Network, and Open Society-linked grant channels. Their full-time staff conducted over 700 advocacy meetings across California in a single legislative cycle. The lobbying infrastructure they built remains fully intact and active.

Active Campaigns

New York A6920/S6531 would add caste to state employment and housing anti-discrimination law. As of the close of the 2025–26 session, it carried 22 Assembly and 11 Senate co-sponsors and an endorsement from the UN Special Rapporteur on minority issues. CoHNA and HAF filed formal opposition. It returns in January 2027.

Advocacy groups have pushed U of M to adopt caste discrimination policies. Michigan is where A4H's chapter is most active — and this is exactly why local organization matters. We need to be at the table before this vote happens, not after.

Faculty and student groups have pushed to add caste to Harvard's non-discrimination policy. The campaign is ongoing — and the framework being used is identical to what passed in Seattle.

Separately, a 2026 Columbia Law Review article proposes adding caste to federal employment law under Title VII. If adopted as an academic precedent, it sets the stage for federal litigation and eventual rulemaking — making caste discrimination a nationwide federal employment law issue affecting every Hindu American professional in the country.

The Michigan Proof Point

In 2024, 101,000 Michigan voters cast "Uncommitted" ballots in the Democratic primary — a coordinated protest vote organized by a single community. Michigan was decided by roughly 80,000 votes in the general election. That community, without a large PAC, demonstrated that organized bloc power moves electoral outcomes.

The pattern works. It works for every community that commits to it. The only question is whether we are willing to do what they did.
