The Open Door: Why Hindu and Indian-American Michiganders Should Show Up for Haley Stevens on August 4

A community that has built temples, schools, and businesses across this state has rarely organized around a single ballot line. This primary is a test of whether that changes.

By Anand Sinha, Political Strategist
Michigan's U.S. Senate Primary is August 4, 2026 Open primary — no party registration required to vote. Early/absentee voting begins in late July.

Every two years, Hindu and Indian-American families across Oakland, Wayne, and Macomb counties write checks to temples, sponsor scholarships, and fill banquet halls for cultural events. Far fewer show up, organized and informed, to a primary ballot. On August 4, that has to change — and the case starts with a simple, verifiable question: when a sitting member of Congress has actually used her office on behalf of Hindu Americans, does the community notice, or does it stay home?

Michigan's 2026 U.S. Senate primary is one of the most consequential Democratic contests in the country this cycle, a genuinely competitive three-way race between Rep. Haley Stevens, state Sen. Mallory McMorrow, and former Wayne County health official Abdul El-Sayed for the seat being vacated by Gary Peters. National Democrats, unions, and outside groups have poured tens of millions of dollars into it. What gets less attention is that Michigan runs an open primary — any registered voter can request a Democratic ballot on August 4 regardless of party affiliation. That single fact is what makes community-wide participation possible, and why this is a civic argument first, not a partisan one. A Hindu-American Michigander who has never voted in a Democratic primary in their life can walk in on August 4 and have a voice in this race.

The record, not the rhetoric

Politicians say a great deal at cultural events. What matters more is what shows up in the congressional record — bills, resolutions, committee assignments, caucus leadership. On that narrower and more honest standard, Stevens has a file worth examining.

H.Res. 1131 — 118th Congress

A bipartisan House resolution celebrating Hindu Americans and explicitly condemning attacks on Hindu places of worship, Hinduphobia, and anti-Hindu bigotry. Stevens is recorded among its supporters.

Source: LegiScan, H.Res. 1131

H.Res. 1085 — 116th Congress

A resolution condemning terrorist attacks and religious persecution against Hindus and Sikhs in Afghanistan, and supporting their resettlement as refugees in the United States — welcomed publicly by the Hindu American Foundation as a humanitarian milestone.

Source: LegiScan; Hindu American Foundation press release

Vice Chair, American Sikh Congressional Caucus (117th Congress)

A formal leadership role in the caucus built to educate Congress on Sikh-American civil rights, religious liberty, and hate-crime concerns — issues that overlap closely with Hindu-American priorities on temple security, bullying, and public mischaracterization of Dharmic traditions.

Source: Ballotpedia, Sikh Congressional Caucus

Committee Portfolio: Science, Education, and U.S.–China Strategic Competition

Stevens sits on committees touching STEM policy, education and workforce development, and the Select Committee on Strategic Competition with the Chinese Communist Party — the exact policy terrain where Indian-American families' interests in immigration pipelines, high-skill careers, and national competitiveness actually get decided.

Source: stevens.house.gov, Committees and Caucuses

On the question community organizations are right to ask before any endorsement — does caucus involvement with Sikh-American groups imply any sympathy for Khalistan separatism or Sikhs for Justice — the honest answer, after review, is no. The Sikh Congressional Caucus's stated mission is civil rights, education, and religious liberty, not separatism, and no public record connects Stevens to SFJ or a Khalistan referendum. That distinction matters, and it should be stated plainly rather than left to insinuation.

A community that wants its concerns understood cannot outsource the job of explaining them. Stevens has shown she will listen. What she does with that is now partly up to us.

Why "good enough" is actually the right standard

It would overstate the case to call Stevens the leading national champion of Hindu-American issues in Congress. She is not. She has not authored a standalone bill on anti-Hindu hate crimes, has not led hearings on temple vandalism, and has not built a comprehensive Hindu-American legislative agenda. Any honest article has to say that clearly, because exaggerating a record helps no one and damages the credibility of the people making the case.

But that is precisely the point a community organizing for the first time around a Senate race needs to understand. Members of Congress operate under brutal constraints on time, staff, and political capital. They prioritize communities that are organized, visible, and persistent — not necessarily the ones that are most successful in medicine or engineering. Professional achievement does not automatically convert into political access. Civic participation does. Stevens has used real institutional mechanisms — resolutions, caucus leadership, committee positioning — on matters that touch Hindu and Sikh communities directly. That is a documented starting point, not a finish line, and the strategic question for Hindu-American voters is what happens if that engagement is rewarded with a Senate seat and deepened, versus what happens if it is ignored.

What the record doesn't show — stated plainly

  • No authored federal legislation specifically targeting Hinduphobia as a standalone bill.
  • No hearings led specifically on anti-Hindu hate crimes or temple vandalism.
  • No comprehensive, published Hindu-American policy platform.
  • Scrutiny exists over corporate PAC and pro-Israel group support, and over retained SpaceX-linked PAC contributions — fair questions for any voter to weigh alongside the rest of the record.

In a crowded, well-funded three-way race, primaries are frequently decided by turnout in specific communities and precincts, not by statewide name recognition alone. A Hindu and Indian-American electorate that has historically sat out primaries has, almost by definition, more relative leverage in a close multi-candidate race than it does in a head-to-head general election where the major-party choice is already locked in. That is not a partisan argument. It is a turnout-math argument, and it is exactly the kind of organized, repeatable participation the dossier behind this article calls "the only thing that reliably converts sympathy into policy."

The generational stake

None of this is really about one Senate term. It is about whether the next generation grows up in a Michigan where temple vandalism gets a same-day congressional response, where school curricula represent Hinduism accurately, where Hindu and Sikh students aren't bullied without anyone in power noticing, and where elected officials have already met the community before a crisis forces an introduction. Symbolic resolutions become briefings. Briefings become letters. Letters become hearings. None of that ladder gets climbed by an official who has never heard from the community in the first place — and it does not get climbed by a community that disappears between election cycles.

Show up on August 4 — and stay engaged after

Michigan's open primary means party registration is not a barrier. Confirm your polling location, consider an absentee ballot if you'll be traveling, and bring family members who have never voted in a primary before.

The honest bottom line

Haley Stevens has not earned a blank check from Hindu-American Michiganders, and no candidate should be handed one on the strength of a few resolutions and a caucus title. What she has earned is a fair hearing based on a real, if still developing, record — and the community's obligation, win or lose on August 4, is to keep showing up with specific asks, evidence, and follow-through long after the ballots are counted. That is what turns a sympathetic vote in Congress into durable policy. It starts with a single afternoon at the polls.
