A4H Endorses Haley Stevens for U.S. Senate

This isn't about what we owe Haley Stevens. It's about what we owe ourselves — to keep, support, and grow the few officials who already stand with our community, for our kids, our grandkids, our temples, our festivals, and our right to our own sacred symbols.

Vote in Michigan's Open Primary on August 4, 2026 No party registration required — every Hindu American in Michigan, regardless of party affiliation, can vote in this primary
Executive Summary 60-second read

A4H has endorsed Haley Stevens in Michigan's August 4 Democratic U.S. Senate primary, based on her documented record on issues affecting Hindu Americans.

Stevens backed H.Res. 1131, condemning Hinduphobia and attacks on Hindu temples, and H.Res. 1085, condemning persecution of Hindus and Sikhs in Afghanistan. She served as Vice Chair of the Sikh Congressional Caucus and sits on committees covering STEM, education, and U.S.–China competition — issues central to Indian-American families.

Political scientists have long observed that communities that organize, vote, donate, and remain engaged tend to receive greater sustained attention from elected officials — a dynamic sometimes described as the political participation feedback loop. The lesson isn't to reward Stevens for past favors — it's to retain and nurture one of the few officials willing to stand with us. Public officials are more likely to continue investing political capital in communities that remain civically engaged over time.

The ask is simple: regardless of your party affiliation, request a Democratic ballot on August 4 and vote for Haley Stevens. Michigan's open primary makes this possible for every Hindu American voter in the state.

"She puts us on the table when her opponents are leaving us off the menu."

A4H doesn't ask the community to trust a press release. We ask the community to check our work. What follows is the evidence behind this endorsement — factual sections first, our recommendation clearly labeled when we get there.

Why politicians invest political capital: civic engagement and legislative attention

Members of Congress do not have unlimited time or political capital. Every year, thousands of bills are introduced, hundreds of advocacy organizations seek meetings, and countless communities ask elected officials for support. Political science suggests that legislators generally allocate their limited time and attention based on a combination of factors:

Factors shaping legislative attention

Constituent needs — the visible, documented concerns of people in their district or state.
Civic engagement — whether a community votes, donates, volunteers, and shows up consistently over time.
Coalition building — whether the community is part of broader coalitions that create legislative opportunity.
Personal conviction — the official's own values and prior experience with a community.
National policy priorities — whether an issue aligns with the broader legislative agenda.
Legislative opportunity — whether the political moment creates space to act on an issue.

Framework derived from: Mayhew, D. (1974). Congress: The Electoral Connection; Fenno, R. (1978). Home Style: House Members in Their Districts

Political scientists have long observed that communities which organize, vote, donate, volunteer, and remain engaged tend to receive greater sustained attention from elected officials — a dynamic sometimes described as the political participation feedback loop. This doesn't mean politicians ignore other communities out of malice. Rather, they have limited resources and tend to invest more effort where they see both need and sustained civic engagement.

The purpose of this endorsement is therefore not merely to document Haley Stevens' record. It is to ask whether she has invested measurable political capital in issues affecting Hindu Americans, and whether that engagement has been sustained over time. Public officials are more likely to continue that investment in communities that remain civically engaged — which is precisely why A4H's ask on August 4 is also an investment in every election and policy conversation that comes after it.

Why this matters: retaining the allies we already have

That lens is the one A4H asks every Hindu American in Michigan to use on August 4. Think about yourselves. Think about your kids. Think about your grandkids — and the Michigan they inherit, where temples are protected, festivals are respected on the public calendar, and our sacred symbols, including the swastika, are understood for what they actually mean to us rather than confused with a hate symbol that has nothing to do with our faith. None of that happens by accident. It happens because a community keeps, and grows, the officials willing to learn it and act on it.

Why we endorsed her: the record

A4H does not endorse on the basis of a single warm conversation or a Diwali greeting. We endorse on the basis of formal, checkable congressional action — the kind that shows up in the record whether or not anyone is watching.

H.Res. 1131 — 118th Congress

A bipartisan House resolution celebrating Hindu Americans and explicitly condemning attacks on Hindu places of worship, Hinduphobia, and anti-Hindu bigotry. Stevens is recorded among its supporters.

Sources: LegiScan — H.Res. 1131 · Congress.gov — Full Resolution Text

H.Res. 1085 — 116th Congress

A resolution condemning terrorist attacks and religious persecution against Hindus and Sikhs in Afghanistan, and supporting their resettlement as refugees in the United States — welcomed publicly by the Hindu American Foundation as a humanitarian milestone.

Sources: LegiScan — H.Res. 1085 · Congress.gov — Full Resolution Text · Hindu American Foundation press release

Vice Chair, American Sikh Congressional Caucus (117th Congress)

A formal leadership role in the caucus built to educate Congress on Sikh-American civil rights, religious liberty, and hate-crime concerns — issues that overlap closely with Hindu-American priorities on temple security, bullying, and public mischaracterization of Dharmic traditions.

Sources: Ballotpedia — Sikh Congressional Caucus · stevens.house.gov — Caucus Memberships

Committee Portfolio: Science, Education, and U.S.–China Strategic Competition

Stevens sits on committees touching STEM policy, education and workforce development, and the Select Committee on Strategic Competition with the Chinese Communist Party — the exact policy terrain where Indian-American families' interests in immigration pipelines, high-skill careers, and national competitiveness actually get decided.

Sources: stevens.house.gov — Committees and Caucuses · Congress.gov — Member Profile

On a question A4H specifically vetted before endorsing — whether caucus involvement with Sikh-American groups implies any sympathy for Khalistan separatism or Sikhs for Justice — our review found no evidence connecting Stevens to SFJ or a Khalistan referendum. The caucus's stated mission is civil rights, education, and religious liberty, not separatism. We are stating that distinction explicitly because we expect the community to ask, and we'd rather answer it than have it answered for us.

Why a three-way primary makes our vote matter more

Michigan's Democratic Senate primary is a genuinely competitive three-way race. In contests this close, organized turnout from specific communities can be decisive in a way it rarely is in a two-candidate general election. A Hindu and Indian-American electorate that shows up together, on one ballot line, on one date, has more leverage in this primary than it will have in almost any other election this decade.

The generational stake

This endorsement isn't about one Senate term. It's about whether the next generation of Hindu-American kids in Michigan grows up with elected officials who are already familiar with this community and positioned to engage when issues arise — whether that means temple vandalism, school curriculum, or the misrepresentation of sacred symbols. That ladder — symbolic resolution to briefing to letter to hearing to policy — only gets climbed by an official the community has already engaged, and only by a community that shows up when it counts. August 4 is when it counts.
